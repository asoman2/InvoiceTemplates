01 · The big idea

What is construction billing?

Why large projects need a structured, staged payment system — and how it protects everyone involved.

Think of it this way

Imagine hiring a contractor to build your house. Instead of handing over all the money upfront, you agree to pay in stages — a little at a time, as each part of the work is genuinely completed.

Before work begins, the contractor gives you a to-do list with a price tag on every task: clearing the land costs $50,000, the foundation costs $120,000, the roof costs $80,000. Every month, they send a bill saying: "I finished one-third of the foundation — please pay me $40,000."

There's also a built-in safety net: you hold back 10% of each payment until the entire project is done and you're satisfied. That withheld money is called retainage — it protects you if something goes wrong at the very end.

On million-dollar projects, this monthly process involves many people, hundreds of line items, and strict paperwork rules. The strategy document describes software that makes all of this automatic, accurate, and fully auditable.

02 · The people involved

Four roles in every payment cycle

Each payment application passes through four people. Each has a distinct job — and a distinct worry.

🏢
The Owner
The client — writing the cheques

They're funding the entire project. Their central question is: is the work actually done before I release any money? They won't approve anything without an independent sign-off from the Architect first.

Am I being billed for work that hasn't been finished?
✍️
The Architect
The independent referee — their signature unlocks payment

They designed the building and now physically visit the site to verify what's being claimed is actually there. Their signature is what releases the money. If something doesn't check out, the whole application comes back for revision.

If I approve something that isn't done, my professional reputation is on the line.
👷
The Subcontractor
The specialist doing the actual work

The plumbing crew, the electricians, the steel erectors — whoever did the physical work. They need to get paid on time because they're already paying their own workers out of pocket while waiting. A single paperwork error can delay payment by weeks.

If I fill the form out wrong, payment is delayed while my crew still needs wages.
📈
The Billing Clerk
The numbers person — the last check before submission

Working behind the scenes at the main construction company. They verify every calculation, confirm the retainage percentage is exactly right, and make sure every required document is attached. One error and the whole package gets kicked back to start.

A rounding error or missing document means restarting the whole submission.

03 · The billing table

How to read a Schedule of Values

The SOV is the master price list for the project. Here's what each column means in plain English.

What you're looking at

Each row is one task on the project. The columns answer: what was agreed as the cost, what's already been billed, what's being claimed this month, and how much is being held back as a safety deposit.

Two things to notice

Site preparation is 100% done, but $5,000 is still held. That safety deposit only releases when the entire project is finished and accepted — not just this one task. That's the whole point of retainage.

The drainage work wasn't in the original plan. When it came up, both parties agreed a price and signed a change order — a formal amendment. It immediately joins the billing cycle and is tracked just like everything else.

05 · Glossary

Key terms, plain and simple

Terms you'll encounter throughout the strategy document, translated into everyday language.

SOV
Schedule of Values
The master to-do list with a dollar value on every task. All billing must tie back to this list — nothing can be billed that isn't on it.
Retainage
The safety deposit
Typically 10% of each payment held back by the owner until the full project is done and accepted. Protects against defects or unfinished work at the very end.
AIA G702 / G703
The standard billing forms
Industry-standard documents. G702 is the one-page summary. G703 is the detailed line-by-line breakdown. Together they form the monthly payment application.
Change Order
A formal amendment
When unexpected work arises, a change order records the new scope and agreed price, and formally adds it to the SOV so it can be billed.
Pay Application
The monthly invoice package
The full bundle submitted each billing period — the G702 summary, G703 detail sheet, lien waivers, and all supporting documents.
Lien Waiver
A "paid in full" receipt
A legal document signed by subcontractors confirming they've been paid and won't place a legal claim against the property for that amount.
RBAC
Permission levels
Role-Based Access Control. A billing clerk can fill in numbers but can't change the template design — like a bank teller who can process transactions but can't change bank policy.
WIP / WBS
Work tracker / task list
Work-in-Progress and Work Breakdown Structure. Internal tools tracking what's done, started, and not yet begun — feeding directly into the billing system.
